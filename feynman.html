The Feynman Technique, named after physicist Richard Feynman, is a method for mastering complex ideas by explaining them in the simplest way possible. If you can't explain a concept simply, you don't really understand it. This technique pushes you to confront gaps in your knowledge, deepen understanding, and build confidence in what you've learned.

STRUCTURE

Ideal For: Complex theories, conceptual subjects, formulas, and real-world applications

Tools: Notebook, whiteboard, digital docs

Time Required: 30 minutes to 1 hour per topic

Complements: Active Recall and Interleaved Practice

Steps to Master Feynman

STEP 1

Choose a concept you want to learn

Pick a topic you're currently studying—something that feels difficult or unclear. This could be anything from "Ohm's Law" to "Supply and Demand" or "Recursion in Programming."

STEP 2

Explain it as if you're teaching a 12-year-old

Write or speak about the topic in simple terms. Avoid jargon. Pretend you're explaining it to a child or someone completely unfamiliar with it. Use analogies, visuals, and real-life examples if helpful.

STEP 3

Identify the gaps in your explanation

Where did you get stuck? What part did you skip or explain vaguely? These are signs you haven't fully grasped that part. Go back to your textbook or resources and revisit those points.

STEP 4

Simplify and refine

Now that you've filled in the gaps, rewrite or re-explain the concept even more clearly. Your goal is to create a version that's so simple, you could teach it to anyone—without losing the core meaning.

STEP 5

Review and repeat

Practice teaching it out loud. If possible, explain it to a friend or classmate and invite questions. If they understand and can repeat it back, you've nailed it. If not, revise and repeat the process.

Congratulations, you have mastered Feynman!
